HAS Flows Metrics

The analysis of the results obtained from the supervised experiments highlights that the number of requests for audio and video resources remains fairly constant, both under conditions of limited bandwidth and when the event is distributed through TCP or UDP protocols. This behavior is attributable to the fact that, from an application perspective, audio and video streams are segmented into regular portions of predefined duration (about 2 seconds for platforms like DAZN). The temporal segmentation is independent of the quality of the video representations, which is why the number of segments that make up an event remains unchanged even as the quality of the transmitted content varies.

However, it is observed that the quality of the video significantly increases with the increase in available bandwidth. When the bandwidth transitions from limited conditions (identified by a dark red color) to more favorable conditions (indicated by a light green color), the quality of the video increases substantially, measured in Kilobits per second (Kbps). This behavior is expected and consistent with the characteristics of the DASH (Dynamic Adaptive Streaming over HTTP) protocol.

In contrast, the quality of the audio shows no significant variations in relation to the bandwidth. This behavior can be explained by the fact that audio, compared to video, requires less data to maintain an acceptable quality level for the user. Even under conditions of limited bandwidth, the quality of the audio remains stable and satisfactory, without the need for frequent or drastic adjustments, as occurs for video streams. This confirms that the perceived quality of audio tends to be less sensitive to network changes compared to video, as users are often willing to accept lower quality audio without significantly affecting the overall experience.

Volumetric Analysis

From an application perspective, the client is responsible for generating HTTP messages in which it requests the download of a video or audio segment or the next manifest (HTTP message with a GET method, with no body). The server, in turn, is required to respond with a message containing the requested data (HTTP response message with a variable-size body). If the quality of the video is very high, the spatial density of the pixels is greater (240p, 360p, 480p, etc.), and therefore the size of each frame increases accordingly. It is expected that when the quality of the video is high, the volume of data sent from the server in the TCP or UDP stream reaching the client will reflect the size of the requested frames. In the following graphs, we have represented the evolution over time of the magnitude describing the number of bytes transported by the protocol in both directions. Subsequently, we used the CDF to represent the same magnitude in the probability space.

As bandwidth changes, the identifier of the requested DASH resource representation changes for the client, but the physical size of the message does not change. This means that, regardless of the configured bandwidth, the TCP and UDP stream from the client to the server will always carry the same number of bytes. If this aspect is quite clear in TCP and widely verifiable from the following graphs, the same cannot be said for UDP. In UDP, it is indeed not possible to clearly distinguish which datagrams carry the HTTP messages, as the acknowledgment bytes of the QUIC protocol are also encapsulated in UDP. Therefore, while in TCP the represented size reflects 1:1 the size of the HTTP messages (net of the overhead from the TLS protocol), in UDP, the acknowledgment variable is also present. Furthermore, as long as the MTU (Maximum Transfer Unit) on the path does not change, the allocatable space in each segment or datagram is constant. The collateral effect of reproducing high-quality video is not only that it requires larger volumes to download but also increases the overhead from the protocol through a greater number of headers. This fact results in an increase in acknowledgment packets, both in TCP and in UDP, and explains the absence of a complete overlap of the curves describing the volumes emitted by the client under different bandwidth conditions, as it is not possible to separate the two types of datagrams in UDP.

Temporal Analysis

From the graph showing the evolution of the average duration of bins, it is discovered that the playback process of the DAZN event always maximizes the quality of the playable video. For this reason, the percentage of free bandwidth compared to that occupied is always very small in all cases where there is no surplus of bandwidth. This is evident because the average duration of the bins overlaps across all bandwidths except for the supervised experiments in which no limitation is foreseen.

In all cases where the ratio between free bandwidth and occupied bandwidth is very low, the data exchange between client and server is not impulsive: the download time for all bandwidths is constant because the download speed increases proportionally to the volume to be downloaded.
